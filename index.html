The New 7 Wonders of the World

Introduction to the New 7 Wonders of the World

The New 7 Wonders of the World were chosen in 2007 through a global poll conducted by the New7Wonders Foundation. These iconic landmarks represent human creativity and engineering brilliance across different civilizations. Each wonder has its own unique history and significance, showcasing the diverse cultural heritage of our planet.

The Great Wall of China Petra, Jordan Christ the Redeemer Machu Picchu Chichen Itza The Colosseum Taj Mahal

The Great Wall of China

The Great Wall of China, one of the world’s most extraordinary architectural achievements, stretches for more than 21,000 kilometers across rugged mountains and deserts and was constructed over many centuries to protect ancient Chinese kingdoms from invasions; today, it stands as a symbol of strength and endurance, earning its place as one of the New Seven Wonders of the World.

Petra, Jordan

Petra, located in the deserts of southern Jordan, is an ancient Nabatean city carved into rose-red sandstone cliffs more than 2,000 years ago; it was once a major trading hub connecting Arabia, Egypt, and the Mediterranean, and is famous for its stunning rock-cut architecture like the Treasury and the Monastery, making it one of the most spectacular archaeological sites and one of the New Seven Wonders of the World.

Christ the Redeemer, Brazil

Christ the Redeemer, standing 30 meters tall atop Mount Corcovado in Rio de Janeiro, Brazil, is an iconic statue of Jesus Christ that overlooks the city with open arms, symbolizing peace, faith, and hope; completed in 1931, it is one of the most recognized landmarks in the world and proudly holds its title as one of the New Seven Wonders of the World.

Machu Picchu, Peru

Machu Picchu, a breathtaking 15th-century Incan citadel nestled high in the Andes Mountains of Peru, showcases the ingenuity of Incan engineering with its terraces, temples, and intricate stone structures; often called the “Lost City of the Incas,” it offers stunning panoramic views and deep cultural significance, securing its place as one of the New Seven Wonders of the World.

Chichen Itza, Mexico

Located in Mexico’s Yucatán Peninsula, was an important Maya city known for its impressive architecture and advanced astronomical knowledge; its most famous structure, El Castillo (Temple of Kukulcán), is a step pyramid with 365 steps representing the solar year and during the equinoxes, the setting sun creates a serpent-like shadow on its staircase, making it a remarkable example of Mayan science and culture; the site also includes the Great Ball Court and sacred cenotes, and it was declared a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1988 and named one of the New Seven Wonders of the World in 2007.

The Colosseum, Italy

The Colosseum in Rome, Italy, is an enormous oval amphitheater built during the Roman Empire nearly 2,000 years ago, designed to host gladiatorial contests, animal hunts, and public spectacles for tens of thousands of spectators; as a remarkable feat of ancient engineering and a lasting symbol of Roman culture, it remains one of the greatest monuments of antiquity and one of the New Seven Wonders of the World.

Taj Mahal, India

The Taj Mahal, located in Agra, India, is a magnificent white marble mausoleum built by the Mughal emperor Shah Jahan in the 17th century in memory of his beloved wife Mumtaz Mahal; admired for its perfect symmetry, intricate carvings, and breathtaking beauty, it is considered the ultimate symbol of eternal love and is rightfully celebrated as one of the New Seven Wonders of the World.
